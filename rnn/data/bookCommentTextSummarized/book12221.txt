Instead of going through the motions of an alienated character who was unconvincing then and is stereotypical now, the earlier Meursault looks for something that resembles religion, a form of pantheistic oneness of self and world.
What he wants comes very close to Spengler's definition of religion: a tension between man and the universe that man can love.
That's the sort of thing philosophical novelists give us to love: a tension.

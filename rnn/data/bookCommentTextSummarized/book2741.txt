The narrator is so peripheral to his telling as to be void of personality.
When his friend, Jacques's plane does not arrive at its African postal destination the narrator recalls their young fascination with a young lady and the brief, nearly non-existent, affair Jacques had with her when she nearly left her dysfunctional marriage.
The sad, sordid relationship in the middle of the book, the "meat" of the novel, is the weakest part--it rings the least true, and you feel that the author is much more at home among the sand and stars of the Sahara than in the salons and parks of Paris.Still, the writing is dreamy and beautiful, as ever, and the descriptions of piloting small planes back and forth across the Mediterranean in the 1920s makes jaw-dropping good reading.
Starting as a mail plane’s journey from France to Africa, both the pilot and the narrator contemplate life and love and the childhood they shared, as the story develops into a tale of love and loss.The language in this novel is beautiful, although that was no surprise to me, as I’m familiar with several of his works.
I experienced a feeling of melancholic longing both for my childhood and the Golden Age of 1920s.And this is why I gave this novel the highest rating.
There are some writers whom I would love no matter what they wrote about, purely because they can create something beautiful with even the most hideous of words.

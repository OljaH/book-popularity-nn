John Williams’ first book is a quick read and not just because it’s a novella but because Williams was a good writer right out the gate and his prose is very smooth and accessible.
In this New York Review of Books edition, there’s an interview included at the back with Williams’ widow, Nancy, who reveals that he wrote this short book at just 22 years old while serving in the Air Force during WW2 (this was the only way he could go to university - to have the government pay his way in exchange for service in the war); an impressive fact alone.
I’ve read John Williams’s novel  Stoner three or four times; I’ve studied it and consider it a perfect novel.
This novella, 123 pages, first published in 1948 and written by twenty-two-year-old Williams when he was enlisted in the Army Air Force, portends the talent of Stoner and Williams’s other books, so it is worth reading.
But it is young, overly earnest, self-conscious, and sometimes even melodramatic—as are many young writers’ first novels; this is not a criticism, just a fact of immaturity.
I can’t find the reference, but I think I recall reading that Williams wanted to bury this novel, and I understand why.
The much celebrated author of "Stoner" and "The Butcher's Crossing" failed to fully accomplish the purpose of his first novel's premise.
The prose is boringly wordy and somewhat lost in endless descriptions that subtract the desired effect of the substance.
Perhaps because the novel takes place in that thin line that separates dream from reality and Williams feared to lose the thread.
But the thread lost itself in the fear of the grip.
I feel frustrated for not seeing the potential of "Nothing but the Night" fully translated into the prose.
A more mature Williams would have made a remarkable novel out of this "draft-of-a-thing-to-be".

The Bicentennial Man is a robot story in Asimov’s robot-verse. It’s not about the 3 laws or the emergence of consciousness, but rather about the rights of robots and what defines us as human beings.
In this case, that is of course the question whether robots can be considered 'human' if they obtain abilities that surpass their basic programming.Asimov is way too preachy and keeps hammering on about his three laws of robotics.
Their only function is to propel a story and have robots going: "Oh no, now I must do something that is in conflict with the second and third law, but if I don't I break the first law, help my positronic brain explodes!" Really, a rule that says: "Robots must always obey humans", what practical use does it have when one guy goes: "Robbie, pick up that ashtray" and the other guy says at the same time: "Robbie you better leave that ashtray alone!""Bicentennial man" is a rather lame result of an idea that already wasn't sooo fascinating to start with.

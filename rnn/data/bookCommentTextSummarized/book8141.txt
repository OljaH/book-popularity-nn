Still loyal to Stalin and the party, Olga Petrovna lives a life in constant fear at both work and home.

The social realist, small-scale, human, truthful drama of a woman coming to terms with the abduction, by the Stalinist state, of her son.
Its power lies in its mundanity―in its view of workplace politics, of state-sponsored peer pressure, of mothers queuing for hours/days/months for the merest tidbits of information.
It has every appearance of complete truthfulness to life.
